Health Insurance Claims Process Guide
Synthetic operational template for training and workflow testing
Purpose. This guide describes a standard end-to-end process for receiving, validating, adjudicating, paying, denying, and appealing health insurance claims. It is suitable as a sample input for document extraction, workflow automation, and knowledge-base testing.
1. Roles and Responsibilities
2. Standard Claims Workflow
Receive and log: Accept electronic or paper claims. Record date received, member ID, provider ID, service dates, billed amount, and claim type.
Validate required data: Check for member identifier, patient relationship, diagnosis codes, procedure codes, place of service, units, provider identifiers, and itemized charges.
Confirm eligibility: Verify that coverage was active on each date of service and that the patient was eligible under the contract.
Check duplicates and coordination of benefits: Identify exact or near-duplicate claims and determine whether another payer is primary.
Apply authorization and medical-policy rules: Confirm required referrals or prior authorizations. Route complex cases for clinical review.
Price the claim: Apply contracted rates, fee schedules, bundling edits, deductible, copayment, coinsurance, and out-of-pocket accumulation.
Adjudicate: Approve, partially approve, pend, or deny each service line. Record clear reason codes.
Issue payment and explanation: Send payment to the correct payee and produce an explanation of benefits or remittance advice.
Handle adjustments and appeals: Correct processing errors, recover overpayments where appropriate, and conduct timely appeal reviews.
3. Intake Checklist
Claim received date is captured.
Member and patient identifiers match enrollment records.
Provider name, tax identifier, and national provider identifier are present.
Service dates are valid and not in the future.
Diagnosis and procedure codes are syntactically valid.
Billed charges and units are numeric and internally consistent.
Attachments are present when required.
Potential duplicate claims are flagged.

5. Common Denial and Pend Reasons
Eligibility: Coverage was inactive or the patient was not enrolled on the service date.
Prior authorization: A required authorization was missing, expired, or did not match the service.
Medical necessity: Submitted records did not support the service under the applicable policy.
Coding: Diagnosis, procedure, modifier, units, or place-of-service information was invalid or inconsistent.
Timely filing: The claim was received after the fictional 180-day filing limit.
Coordination of benefits: Another insurer may be primary and additional information is needed.
Duplicate: The same or substantially similar service was previously processed.
6. Appeals Process
1.  Submit a written appeal within 180 calendar days of the adverse determination.
2.  Include the claim number, member name, service dates, disputed services, and the requested resolution.
3.  Attach relevant medical records, authorization evidence, coding documentation, or proof of timely filing.
4.  Acknowledge receipt within 5 business days.
5.  Complete a standard appeal within 30 calendar days unless additional time is allowed by law or contract.
6.  Send a written determination explaining the evidence reviewed, decision, and any further review rights.
7. Quality-Control Rules
Every denial must map to a documented rule and a member-friendly explanation.
Clinical reviewers must be appropriately qualified for the issue reviewed.
No employee may approve a payment to themselves or alter audit logs.
All manual overrides require a reason and supervisor approval.
Protected health information should be limited to the minimum necessary.
Processing timeliness, overturn rate, rework rate, and financial accuracy should be monitored monthly.
